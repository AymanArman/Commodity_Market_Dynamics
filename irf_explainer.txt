IMPULSE RESPONSE FUNCTIONS (IRFs) — COMPLETE EXPLAINER
========================================================

WHAT IS AN IRF?
---------------
An Impulse Response Function shows how a variable (the "responder") reacts over time
to a sudden shock in another variable (the "impulse"). In the context of this app:

- The impulse is a one standard deviation shock to commodity A (e.g. WTI crude, CL)
- The responders are all other commodities (e.g. BRN, NG, ALI...)
- The X axis is time in trading days following the shock (e.g. day 0 to day 30)
- The Y axis is the magnitude of the response, expressed in standard deviations
- A confidence band (typically 95%) surrounds the response line

The IRF does not show what actually happened on any specific date. It shows the
average expected response pattern based on the historical relationship between
the two series, as estimated by the VAR model.


WHERE DOES THE IRF COME FROM?
------------------------------
The VAR (Vector Autoregression) model estimates how each commodity's current value
depends on its own past values AND the past values of all other commodities in the
system. Once the model is fitted, you simulate: "what if commodity A received a
shock of one standard deviation on day 0 — holding everything else equal — and
we let the system evolve forward?" The resulting trajectory for each other commodity
is the IRF.

Orthogonalised IRFs (Cholesky decomposition) are the standard approach. They assign
causal ordering to the variables, meaning the ordering of tickers in the VAR matters.
Generalised IRFs (Pesaran & Shin) are order-invariant and often preferred for
commodity markets where no strict causal ordering is theoretically justified.


READING THE CHART — PATTERN BY PATTERN
----------------------------------------

1. STRONG POSITIVE RELATIONSHIP
   - Response line rises sharply above zero immediately after day 0
   - Stays above the upper confidence band for several days
   - Gradually decays back toward zero as the shock dissipates
   - Colour: green (outside confidence band)
   - Interpretation: a positive shock in A reliably leads to a positive move in B
   - Example you might expect: CL shock → positive BRN response (both crude benchmarks)

2. STRONG NEGATIVE RELATIONSHIP
   - Response line drops sharply below zero after day 0
   - Stays below the lower confidence band for several days
   - Decays back toward zero
   - Colour: green (outside confidence band, but on the downside)
   - Interpretation: a positive shock in A reliably leads to a negative move in B
   - This is less common in commodity markets but can occur with substitution effects

3. DELAYED RESPONSE
   - Response line stays near zero for the first few days
   - Departs significantly from zero at day 3, 5, or later
   - Confidence band crossed only after the delay
   - Colour: grey early, transitions to green later
   - Interpretation: B does not react immediately to a shock in A — there is a
     transmission lag. This is economically meaningful — it tells you how many
     days it takes for the shock to propagate.
   - Example: a crude shock might take 2-3 days to show up in refined products

4. NO RELATIONSHIP
   - Response line hovers near zero throughout the entire horizon
   - Confidence band straddles zero at every point
   - Line may wiggle slightly due to noise but never departs meaningfully
   - Colour: grey throughout
   - Interpretation: no statistically meaningful predictive relationship exists
     between A and B at any horizon. The model is not forcing a relationship.
   - Example you might expect: CL shock → ALI response (weak or absent channel)

5. TRANSIENT RELATIONSHIP (SHORT-LIVED SHOCK)
   - Response line departs from zero and crosses the confidence band on days 1-3
   - Returns inside the confidence band by day 5-7
   - Colour: green briefly, then grey
   - Interpretation: B reacts to a shock in A but the effect dissipates quickly.
     The market absorbs the shock and returns to normal rapidly.
   - Economically: the two markets are linked but the transmission is not persistent

6. PERSISTENT RELATIONSHIP (LONG-LIVED SHOCK)
   - Response line departs from zero and stays outside the confidence band for
     10, 15, or 20+ days
   - May not fully decay back to zero within the horizon shown
   - Colour: green for a sustained period
   - Interpretation: a shock in A has a lasting effect on B. The markets are
     structurally linked and the effect does not dissipate quickly.
   - This raises questions about whether the series are cointegrated (share a
     long-run equilibrium), which is a separate but related analysis

7. OSCILLATING RESPONSE
   - Response line alternates above and below zero across the horizon
   - May cross the confidence band on both sides at different points
   - Colour: alternates green/grey
   - Interpretation: a shock in A causes B to overshoot and correct repeatedly.
     Can indicate feedback loops or mean-reverting dynamics.
   - Rare but worth flagging if observed — may suggest model misspecification
     or a genuine cyclical transmission mechanism

8. INITIAL SPIKE THEN REVERSAL
   - Response line spikes sharply positive (or negative) on day 1-2
   - Then reverses direction and moves the other way before decaying
   - Colour: green initially, may cross to the other side
   - Interpretation: the immediate reaction is in one direction but the market
     corrects. Could reflect overreaction and mean reversion, or a delayed
     supply/demand adjustment.

9. ASYMMETRIC CONFIDENCE BANDS
   - One side of the confidence band is much wider than the other
   - Interpretation: the model is more certain about the response in one direction
     than the other. High uncertainty in the upside, for example, means positive
     responses are hard to predict precisely even if the central estimate suggests one.


CONFIDENCE BANDS — WHAT THEY ARE AND WHY THEY MATTER
------------------------------------------------------
The confidence band (shaded region around the response line) represents statistical
uncertainty in the estimated IRF. It is typically constructed via bootstrap simulation
— the VAR model is re-estimated many times on resampled data, and the spread of
resulting IRFs gives you the band.

- Narrow band: the model is confident in the estimated response path
- Wide band: high uncertainty — the true response could vary substantially
- Band crossing zero: the response is not statistically distinguishable from zero
  at that horizon

A response line outside the band but with a very wide band should be interpreted
cautiously — the relationship exists but is imprecisely estimated.


COMMON PITFALLS IN INTERPRETATION
-----------------------------------
1. Granger causality ≠ true causation. A predicts B does not mean A causes B.
   A third variable may drive both.

2. The VAR is estimated on historical data. If the structural relationship between
   commodities has changed (e.g. post-shale revolution in US energy markets),
   the IRF reflects the average over the full sample, which may be misleading.

3. Ordering matters for orthogonalised IRFs. If generalised IRFs are not used,
   the results depend on which commodity is placed first in the VAR. This should
   be handled carefully and disclosed.

4. Stationarity assumption. The VAR is estimated on returns (stationary), not
   price levels (non-stationary). IRFs therefore describe return dynamics, not
   price level dynamics.

5. The shock is symmetric by default. A +1 SD shock and a -1 SD shock produce
   mirror-image responses in a linear VAR. Real commodity markets often have
   asymmetric responses (e.g. price spikes vs. price drops behave differently).
   This is a known limitation of linear VAR.


WHAT TO TELL A RISK MANAGER LOOKING AT THESE CHARTS
-----------------------------------------------------
- Green = this relationship is real and statistically meaningful at this horizon
- Grey = no reliable signal; do not trade or hedge on this relationship
- The X axis tells you the speed of transmission — early green means fast propagation
- The Y axis tells you the magnitude — how large is the expected response relative
  to the size of the initial shock
- A persistent green signal means you may need to hedge the responding commodity
  when the impulse commodity moves, not just the impulse commodity itself


GLOSSARY
--------
VAR — Vector Autoregression. A multivariate time series model where each variable
      is regressed on its own lags and the lags of all other variables.

Granger Causality — A statistical test of whether past values of X improve the
                    prediction of Y beyond Y's own history. If yes, X Granger-causes Y.

IRF — Impulse Response Function. The dynamic response of one variable to a shock
      in another, traced out over a defined time horizon.

Orthogonalised IRF — IRF computed after decomposing shocks so they are uncorrelated,
                     using a Cholesky factorisation. Order-dependent.

Generalised IRF — IRF that is invariant to variable ordering. Preferred when no
                  strict theoretical ordering exists.

Confidence Band — The uncertainty interval around the estimated IRF, typically
                  constructed via bootstrap.

AIC — Akaike Information Criterion. Used to select the optimal lag length in the VAR.
      Balances model fit against complexity.

Standard Deviation Shock — The size of the impulse. One SD is the typical unit —
                            it scales the shock to be comparable across commodities
                            regardless of their price levels or units.
